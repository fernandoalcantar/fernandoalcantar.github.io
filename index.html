Who I Am

Mission & Teaching Philosophy

I'm the Director of Student Engagement at East Stroudsburg University — a role I've held for ten years within Campus Life & Inclusive Excellence, overseeing the institution's largest campus-wide traditions and leading a team responsible for the annual programming calendar. I also created Leadership By Design, ESU's professional development certification — a five-track program spanning 14 campus offices. Participants earn credentials across Personal Leadership, Professional Leadership, People Skills, Technical Skills, and Civic Engagement. Within this program I teach multiple sessions: three AI literacy workshops in the Technical Skills certification, workshops in Personal Leadership, Professional Leadership, and People Skills, and Civic Engagement programs participants can complete for points. My sessions focus on AI, psychology, and organizational leadership — my slice of a much larger collaborative curriculum.

I began as a skeptic. Three years ago, I was genuinely uncertain about AI's impact on learning and the future of human connection in education. Through deep engagement — experimenting, questioning, building — I shifted toward becoming an advocate for thoughtful, ethical integration. My own prior research found that 96% of ESU students already use AI tools for academic work, and 84% describe those experiences positively. Faculty and staff readiness has not kept pace. That gap is where my work lives.

As a doctoral candidate, published author, and public speaker, I approach this as a practitioner-researcher. My goal isn't to make everyone an AI expert. It's to help faculty, staff, and administrators move from hesitation to informed curiosity — creating space to explore, question, and reconnect their professional identity with tools that are already reshaping higher education.

Teaching Philosophy
"The goal is not to master everything in one sitting. It's to create space to explore, ask questions, test ideas — and help people see themselves not as passive adopters of technology, but as active, discerning practitioners who can shape how AI is used in their work."

Fast-Access Basics

Quick Reference

A shortcut to the fundamentals — AI lingo, platform comparison, prompting tips, ethics, practical use cases by role, and a ready-to-use prompt library. Useful whether you're prepping for a session or just need a quick answer.

AI Agents
AI that takes actions on your behalf — not just answering questions, but clicking, searching, filling forms, and completing multi-step tasks autonomously.
AI Bias
When AI produces outputs that systematically favor or disadvantage certain groups — often because the training data reflected existing human biases. Critical to understand when using AI in hiring, grading, or advising contexts.
API
Application Programming Interface — a "back door" that lets developers connect AI into their own apps. Universities use APIs to build custom AI-powered tools.
Context Window
The total "working memory" of an AI — how much text it can hold in mind at once. Think of it like a whiteboard the AI works on: everything on it counts.
Fine-Tuning
Training an AI further on specific data to improve performance in a particular domain — like medical records or legal documents.
Generative AI
AI that creates new content — text, images, audio — based on patterns learned from massive amounts of data. ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini are all examples.
Guardrails
Built-in rules that prevent AI from saying harmful or inappropriate things. Different platforms have different levels of strictness.
Hallucination
When AI confidently states something completely made up. It's not lying — it fills gaps in ways that sound plausible but aren't accurate. Always verify important facts.
Iteration
Refining your prompt through multiple exchanges to get closer to what you want. You rarely get a perfect answer on the first try — and that's normal.
Knowledge Cutoff
The date the AI stopped learning new information. It won't know about events after this date unless live web access is enabled.
LLM
Large Language Model — the technical name for the type of AI behind ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini. Trained on enormous amounts of language data.
Max Input
The maximum amount of text you can send to the AI in one session — your prompts, uploaded files, and conversation history all count toward this limit.
Max Output
The maximum length of the AI's response. Even with a huge context window, the AI can only write back a certain amount at a time.
Multimodal
When an AI can handle more than just text — images, audio, video, documents. Gemini and ChatGPT are currently the most fully multimodal.
NLP
Natural Language Processing — the technology that lets AI understand and generate human language naturally, the way you'd speak to a person.
Prompt
The instruction or question you give the AI. Better prompts get better results — the more specific and contextual you are, the closer you get to what you need.
RAG
Retrieval-Augmented Generation — when AI pulls in real-time or specific documents before responding, rather than relying only on training data.
System Prompt
Hidden instructions given to the AI before a conversation starts, used to customize its behavior — e.g., "always respond in an academic tone."
Temperature
A setting controlling creativity vs. precision. High = more creative and varied. Low = more focused and consistent outputs.
Token
A small chunk of text that AI reads and processes — roughly a syllable or word fragment. AI has limits on how many tokens it can handle at once.
Training Data
The massive collection of text the AI learned from — books, websites, articles — which shaped how it responds before you ever talked to it.

1
Be specific about audience and purpose
Vague prompts produce vague results. Tell the AI who you're writing for, what the format should be, and what you're trying to accomplish.
"Write a 50-minute lesson plan on the causes of the Civil War for 10th grade struggling readers. Include one discussion activity and one formative assessment."
2
Specify the format you want
Tell the AI exactly how to structure its output. If you don't specify, the AI decides — and it may not choose what you actually need.
"Give me your response as a numbered list" or "Format this as a table with three columns: Term, Definition, Example."
3
Use iteration — never expect perfection first try
AI works best as a back-and-forth. If the first response isn't right, follow up with specific refinements rather than starting over.
"Make it shorter." / "Use simpler language." / "Add two more examples." / "Rewrite paragraph two to be less formal."
4
Give the AI a role to play
Context helps. Giving the AI a persona often produces more focused, relevant, and appropriately pitched responses for your specific need.
"You are an experienced instructional designer helping a professor restructure a syllabus for online delivery."
5
Always verify important facts
AI can hallucinate — stating something confidently that is simply wrong. Never share AI-generated content with students or colleagues without reviewing it.
Be especially careful with citations, statistics, dates, and any specific claims about people or events.
6
Use it as a first draft, not a final product
AI excels at generating raw material quickly. Use it to draft rubrics, email templates, discussion questions, or reports — then apply your own expertise.
You're still the expert. AI is the first-draft machine.
Privacy & Data
What you type into AI may be stored and used for training future models. Do not enter student names, grades, ID numbers, or any personally identifiable information into public AI tools. Always check your institution's data governance policies first.
Hallucination
AI sounds confident even when it is wrong. Treat AI output like Wikipedia — a useful starting point, not a final source. Especially critical for citations, statistics, legal information, and medical content.
Bias
AI reflects the biases present in its training data. Outputs may skew toward dominant cultural, linguistic, or political perspectives. Interrogate AI responses the same way you would any source — with critical thinking and contextual awareness.
Academic Integrity
A blanket ban on AI is difficult to enforce. A blanket permission creates equity and integrity concerns. The most effective approach: a clear, contextual policy aligned with your course goals — communicated explicitly at the start of the semester.
Transparency
Model critical, transparent engagement with AI in your own workflow. Let students and colleagues know when AI has been part of your process. This normalizes thoughtful use rather than hidden dependence.
Equity & Access
Not all students have equal access to premium AI tools. If AI use is part of an assignment or professional task, confirm whether free-tier tools are sufficient — or whether your institution provides equitable access for everyone involved.

Doctoral Research

Dissertation Research

This workshop portfolio is the applied, real-time expression of ongoing doctoral research at ESU.

Dissertation Title
"Ethical and Pedagogical Applications of Artificial Intelligence in Higher Education"

This research addresses a real gap at ESU: prior study found that 96% of students already use AI tools for academic work — yet faculty and staff readiness has not kept pace. The challenge isn't technical. It's philosophical and psychological. Many educators and administrators approach AI with caution or fear: worrying about academic integrity, the future of human connection in their work, or their own professional relevance. These emotions shape readiness far more than skill gaps.

The study draws on A.J. Juliani's Five-Step Progression to AI Fluency, Action Research methodology (Johnson, 2012; Mertler, 2025), and the principle that fluency develops through exposure, supported experimentation, and authentic practice — not one-time information sessions. Without psychologically safe entry points, adults default to avoidance.

The workshop portfolio here is the applied internship component of this research — translating doctoral inquiry into curriculum serving the institution in real time. Each session generates data: participation patterns, pre-survey responses, verbal reflections, and engagement metrics that feed back into the research cycle.

Research Pillars
1
Ethical UsePrivacy, bias, data governance, and the responsibilities educators and administrators carry when deploying AI.
2
Pedagogical DesignHow AI can be integrated into curriculum, assessment, and feedback in ways that enhance — not replace — human teaching.
3
Faculty & Staff ReadinessWhat people actually need to feel confident using AI — and how professional development meets them where they are.
4
Equity & AccessEnsuring AI adoption in higher education doesn't deepen existing inequities for students, faculty, and staff.

What is an LLM?

A Large Language Model is the engine running underneath most AI assistants. Trained on vast amounts of text, it generates responses by predicting what comes next based on the context you provide. The platform (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini) is what you interact with — the LLM is what's thinking underneath. Different LLMs have different strengths, training data, and design philosophies, which is why the same question can get meaningfully different answers depending on which one you ask.
